RV Datavis: Proof of Concept

Data + Design notes

These notes provide RV with a summary of the design rationale for all three PoC Data visualisation concepts and the findings from reviewing and exploring the context and data that supports the visuals. Context materials includes the design brief (i.e. RFQ/scope of work) and all in-person discussions with RV, as well as:

  • Publicly available data tables used from the release notes for Victoria's Waste Performance and Projection Model and VLGAS data.
  • The Circular Economy Market Reports for 2026 (draft, provided by RV) and prior releases available at the DEECA/RV website (accessed May 2026) to understand the evolution of narrative trends, discussion points, and existing data visuals.
  • The Recycling Victoria Data Hub: to review existing visualisations
  • Publicly available policy documents and strategies related to the Victorian waste targets.

I. Summary of data review observations

These initial observations shaped the data design approach:

  • Available data is heavily focused on collected waste and management. As (also) indicated by RV, this is the natural area of focus for data visuals. The publicly available data tables are well structured and can be used directly (upon conversion to a text file format that can be loaded to the web app).
  • There are interesting 'new' circular economy metrics that could be communicated — namely the material footprint and circularity rate — however their components (i.e. boundaries) are highly nuancedThe concern, in the context of a graphic, is that the these metrics require reasonably detailed explanation and are more suited to commentary. , and there is little detail or series history. This makes them difficult to include at the same depth or resolution as as the waste data, however they remain a candidate for inclusion in the system graphic.
  • Similarly, the 2026 Circular Economy Report includes more focus on valuation and economic measures (related to the waste recovery industry). This analysis is largely confined to the latest yearWe understand that there may be similar analysis that might be extended to prior years, but as its not publicly available, have assumed (in the context of PoC delivery timeframe) that it is not viable to pursue , which makes it a candidate for the System graphic only, as the Journey is time series based.
  • Standard charts (bar and line) and Sankey diagrams feature heavily in existing assets, suggesting that there may be limited value in 'repeating' those types of charts, nor data heavy dashboard-style layouts. All charts also tend to use multiple series for comparison.

  • The use of colour palettes for the eight materials is problematic in any visualisation that requires additional colour palettes for source sector, or branding. Basically, there are too many colours to maintain visual separation and accessibilityIn this case, both contrast in brightness to maintain visual separation from any background colours, and a contrast in hue so as to distinguish each colour used. The implementation of brand colours palette compound this issue as primary coloursFor the EPA (who's brand guide is provided for the PoC DataVis), this includes three shades for blue and a lime green, which covers a fairly wide range of the colour spectrum become 'off limits' for material stream and source sectors.

    The approach taken in the DataVis PoC is to not use any colour palette to separate materials.

II. Design approach

1. The "responsively square" template

All visualisations are designed with a template that includes an HTML sidebar (featuring a header, year selector and annotation/description), and an SVG graphic that has a square aspect ratio. This allows the complete graphic to stack vertically for narrower 'portrait orientated' screensThis is case for mobile phones, tablets, and potentially, if embedded in a narrow content container on a webpage, which is expected for the standard embedding case in a Vic Gov website. In the vertical stacking case, the sidebar can be split into an upper section containing the header, selector and (optional) commentary, and a lower section for (optional) footnote information.

The page aspect ratio has been fixed to the same ratio as A4 page, meaning that graphics print appropriately to paper, and will also maintain their layout and be constrained to the center of widescreen displays.

2. Design language

The design of all visualisations attempts to adopt a consistent approach to the shapes and representations used. The design language leans heavily on the 'circular' vs 'linear' connotation associated with the transition from linear to circular economy, by: representing all positive 'good' data as circles and curves, while using straight lines for the 'linear economy'. The choice of these geometric shapesDesign and art from the modernist De Stijl and Bauhaus movements were used as reference material. Geometric shapes also complement data visualisations as they are familiar marks for charts (e.g. circles and rectangles), and/or are suited to be visually scaled and compared when their size encoded data values. is extended by the use of squares and rectangles (with 'neutral' meaning), and equilateral triangles whose general direction (e.g. up/down) naturally implies meaning.

To add as sense of dynamic movement and to guide the audiences eye, layouts were explored that used bold angles and typography that follows curves and angle paths. These design decisions are intended to provide an bolder set of visuals with a strong silhouette: they deliberately sacrifice some legibility for memorability. Naturally, the bolder layouts are better suited to sparser labelling and detail. The preference for restrained annotation directly on/around hte visualisation is complemented by the sidebar (see 1) which accommodates small blocks of narrative text.

As the (RV) designated 'hero' visual, these ideas were initially explored for the System visualisation, and is then used elsewhere, where appropriate. Detail on how this approach is applied is provide 'by visualisation' below (see section #5).

3. Tone and information density

While each graphic is designed for a broad(er) public audience, it should be acknowledged that this is an audience that is being engaged in a (fairly dry) public policy and data context. Moreover, there is a need for user friendliness, but in the context of a public sector communication, where some design ideas may be restrained or require compromiseLayout options are provided for the System visual, which may provide flexibility for RV to use different versions in different context.

To manage this context, a tone of (draft) copy has been proposed that uses a more casual tone with (slightly more) abstract or curious headings; both intended to encourage a sense of curiosity and avoid the dry/factual tone. This of course, may be completely changed at RV's discretion — being particularly mindful that Government communications must be approved and consistentNote: the RV approved copy will be what is featured; so reference to the proposed draft copy will likely be irrelevant to future readers of this note! For the PoC however, it is not expected that copy will go through external comms process..

The information density of each visualisation must be restrained (vs the available data, particularly the Journey visualisation which takes a highly curated view of its source data). This encourages a 'less is more' approach that favours white/negative space. Moreover, it is generally proposed that content be left out rather than overcrowd the graphic: empty space is a deliberate design decision. Data labelling should also limited to in favour of letting the visual representation and shape of the data tell the storyThe sidebar and footnotes are designed for, and suggested to be used for adding context, guidance and detail, preferably as a last resort.

4. Brand and accessibility

in recognition of RV's transition to EPA Victoria in July 2026, the EPA branding (as provided by DEECA) was applied to the colour palette and typefacesThese are the same for all Victorian Government used in all visualisation. Styling code (CSS) extracted from the Victorian Government websites has also attempted to be used for the visualisation sidebars, however these required some adapting to 'work'Standard styling included paragraph font sizes that were far too large in the context of the visualisation graphics and intended use case of the sidebars, to provide a space for annotation..

The primary EPA palette consists of three shades of blue, and a lime green. In order to integrate the flavour of the brand palette without introducing 'meaning', the blues are used primarily as a base colour, and the green reserved for only in or near context of 'recycled' materials (in the System visual) and 'progress' (in the Targets visual). Moreover, the green is used sparingly, and never for highlighting 'negative' elements.

The use of gradients (as prescribed in the EPA Brand book) and secondary EPA palette are drawn upon where additional colors are requiredA deliberate decision was made to not encode materials with individual colours to avoid a 'rainbow' colour palette that would be difficult to" manage — particularly as source sectors appear alongside — and to maintain brand association...

Critically (from a data design perspective), the primary brand colours — particularly the darkest blue, which is featured extensively — tends to clash darker monochrome colours (i.e. black, charcoal etc.) that are traditionally used in data visualisation as the set of default neutral colours that imply 'no other meaning in the data'. The upshot is that it is difficult to use or incorporate dark monochrome colours, which severely constrains how colours can be appliedThis is particularly evident in the Journey visualisation where the application of brand colours limits the design choice.

Best attempts are made to follow all visual accessibility guidance (font size and contrast) in all graphics, however as the graphics scale to the available screen space, it is difficult (impossible) ensure that font sizes stay are a large enough size on all screensNaturally, labels will never be larger enough on mobile phones screens. However a common issue for all graphics in general (not just data visualisation). In keeping with the information density approach above, there is a design preference to keep data labels smaller and less intrusive: the audiences eyes should be drawn to the data representation and not the data labels.

Other web accessibility concerns (e.g. screen reader) are not addressed in the PoC. Similarly, data accessibility (e.g. providing links to source data) is not included but may be managed by adding a link underneath any embedded visuals.

II. Design notes by concept

i. The System visualisation

This visualisation is designed to capture a view of the Victorian Circular Economy, as a "system". From both the data and context review, it was immediately apparent that waste flows data need to feature prominently in the composition, and that RV had the appropriate datasets highly textured and visually interesting data representation. Considerable attention was given to exploring, and then deciding on, where and how to include the Circular Economy metrics and the market value and economic impact metrics (both introduced in the most recent Circular Economy Market Reports).

Ultimately, the proposed design (provided in various layout modesA total of three modes are provide to RV which play with the angle of the linear economy portion of the graphic. These are known as "d", "b", and "T" to reflect their shape or features" the 'b' mode — which resemebles a balancing beam — is a special case where the recovery rate is used to determine the angle of balance) chooses to:

  • Focus on the 'waste flow' story and highlight the interwoven but contrasting shape of the circular and linear components of the economy.
  • Reference the circular economy metrics (optionally) in the the sidebar annotation and not a visual representation, as this became too cramped and needed more detail to both explain the circular metrics, and its relationship to the waste storyVersions incorporating a graphic showing the proportions of Victoria's TMF, DMC, and SMC (as a circularity rate) were explored but did not reach full design stage: they required extensive explanation and annotation; and clear visual link to waste management graphic to adequately 'connect' concepts. .
  • Include a prominent but (composition-ally) small space for an annotation that references a headline market value/economic impact metric. The rationale here is that there is some uncertainty as to what to include to best tell the story (or if it is reasonable to try and do so); but also to provide some flexibility for how this section might be used Also bearing in mind that these metrics are not available for prior years, so will leave a 'blank space'.

The "waste flows" component consists of a "square of source bands" (blue) that split as connectors to parallel lines that represent each of the eight materials that feature in the datasets. These connectors could be considers as a modified version of a Sankey connectors, where line thickness does represent the relative volume of each source flow to material type To maintain legibility, a reasonable minimum scale is used for thickness, resulting in a visual scale that is linear between minimum and maximum thickness.. The linear flows use a bump chart that ranks the material volumes (from top as highest volume, to bottom as lowest volume), through the chain of disposal/collection to a re-ranking by recovered volumes (before the center, that feed into the circular arc), and onto a final re-ranking by to disposed to landfill volumes.

All connectors and bumps are drawn at a 60° angle to maintain an angular feel that is consistent with the equilateral triangles that are featured.

Flows that split off the circular are then represented by arc lengths that correspond to each materials recovery rate. To emphasis that these flows are representative of the desired behaviour (the Circular Economy), the are given a coloured gradient fill that traverses the EPA colour palette. Conversely, the linear economy components consist colours that are more grey and monotone; and dark at landfill.

The landfill triangle is deliberately designed to point downward in all layout modes. It is sized in proportion to the square of sources, and is visually opposed be an upward pointing triangle that emanates from the center of the recovery circle. This large triangular section is designed as a flexible component to link additional data and information to the circular economy component of the graphic. The proposed use of this section includes a breakdown between local nad exported recovered materials and a segue to a message about the economic impact/size of the Victorian Circular EconomyThe use of text labels in this section reflects the lack of structured and detailed data that might be used here. Moreover, economic 'data metrics' featured in the Circular Economy Market Report are essentially 'headline figures' that lack (enough) context for a data visualisation and are recommended to be include only as headline 'messages' (i.e. not just figures, in order for the audience to be able to comprehend the explicity message)..

An optional set of Bauhaus-styled illustration components can be shown in the visualisation. This is a suggestion that may 'soften' the harsh feel of a data-only graphic. As noted in the user guidance, the illustrations provided in the PoC are free for RV to use 'as is', but may not meet brand and style guidelines (and are not in scope in the PoC for refinement)URLs with the illustrations on and off are provided from the delivered DataVis PoC applications menu page..

Although RV can choose from any of three layout modes, mode "d" is suggested as the most bold and memorable configuration. The rationale here is that this provides the strongest and most memorable silhouette, and was found to have an ideal space to include an additional illustration to represent 'preserved (natural) resources'.

ii. Journey visualisation

This visualisation is designed to tell a story about where Victoria has come from, to where we are now, in terms of (the waste data component) of the circular economy.

As this story is dependent on time series data, only the waste data was considered. This still presented a significant challenge as the series data for waste volume is is broken down by source sector (x3), material stream (x8) and fate (x2), with waste recovery (% by source and material) and per capita volumes (by all volumes); all reasonable candidates for telling the story of Victoria's 'circular economy journey'Two extremes were unsuccessfully explored as the basis for a visualisation (that yielded lessons and direction):
1) A large scale graphic incorporating all waste streams and fates (a modified area bump chart): this highlighted that the order of magnitude difference in scale between material volumes (i.e. dominated by volume, by aggregates, masonry and soils), which makes it difficult to visually see trends and change in most materials; and the problem of trying to surface too many data stories from a single view. In short, this high level view revealed little detail and suggested that a breakdown by series was required to uncover the data stories within.
2) A set of small multiples charts that looked at recovery rates, and then volume broken down by fate (recovered vs disposed to landfill). While there was moderate success in this 'dashboard-style' approach, it was deemed to be (far) too cluttered and confusing for public consumption. Ths exercise did however, highlight the need to curate a focused ser of data to present as a more cohesive story, a the success of a small multiples approach to representing trends and change, at the series level (i.e. change relative to itself, rather than on a common scale with all other materials)..

The final proposed graphic focuses on telling the story of recovered waste, at source and material stream level. It adopts a slightly unconventional variation on a series of sparklines charts, called a ridge line or 'joy' (division) plot that shows series trend data in a compact plot that is designed ot have a small degree of overlap between series.

The aesthetic rationale here is create a sense of curiosity in the audience, and evoke a sense of nature through contours that a reminiscent of patterns of waves, mountains or tree rings. To add to this effect, the relative volume of each material is encoded as line thickness.

To properly contextualise the data for comparison/public consumption (and to present a point of difference to RV Data Hub dashboards), volume data is normalised per capita, in order to present trends that are unaffected by population growth. While this adds an additional layer of complexity/explanation, it is designed to be more relatable to the general public who can then better identify their waste behaviour to the growth trends shown.

The lower section provides small multiple area charts that show the shape of recovery rates over time, for all sectors and materials. As all series are on a common y-scale, area charts are preferred over simpler 'cleaner' lines here to emphasise the comparison between series. THis also provides a visual differentiator to the lines used in the Joy Plots. A gradient filled area (on a common scale) is used to reinforce the scale and make comparison even easier for the audience, while also creating added visual interest.

ii. Target visualisation

This visualisation is designed to show Victoria's progress since 2020, towards four waste targets in 20230. This concept is a relatively straightforward 'info-graphic' style visual that incorporates data into the marks (the 'fans') used to show progress. Instead of the traditional trope of a line chart with target, a four sector fan chart is used as the basis for a 'circular' style visual that honours the circular economy motif, and the design language of geometric shapes.

Data was sourced from both the VMP&PM and VLGAS datasets, with the latter found to provide both population data (for calculating per capita targets), and the counts of LGAs with FOGO services (to support calculations for Target #4).

The end result is a fairly simple data graphic, which mimics the simple goal of showing targets, progress and current position.

The only alternative explored was to use this layout invert the progress to be form outside in (i.e. heading towards the center 'bulls eye'). While elegant in theory however, this resultant graphic proved to be less intuitive that initially thought. Put another way, it sounded good and 'smart' in theory, but just didn't look right in practice!
